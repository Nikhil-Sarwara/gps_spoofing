\chapter{Results and Evaluation}

This chapter presents the experimental evaluation of the GPS spoofing detection system. We characterise the dataset, analyse model performance (including the CNN Paradox), present live inference results from streaming telemetry, examine terrain-aware detection, and quantify detection latency.

\section{Dataset Characterisation}

The experimental dataset comprises 8 simulated flight runs in the PX4 SITL environment with Gazebo. Each flight followed a scripted mission: takeoff to 20\,m altitude, execute a rectangular survey pattern at 5\,m/s cruise speed, and at a predetermined waypoint inject a GPS spoofing attack with a 50\,m north and 30\,m east offset maintained for 30 seconds. \subsection{Environmental Conditions and Model Impact}

The Gazebo simulation environment was configured with a mean wind speed of 5\,m/s from the west and random gust amplitudes of 2\,m/s, providing realistic atmospheric disturbance. Wind affects both GPS ground-track and IMU-detected body motion symmetrically: the same physical displacement registered by both sensors keeps $\Delta\Phi$ within the noise floor, preventing false positives from environmental turbulence. This symmetric coupling is precisely what enables the GPS--IMU consistency discriminator to distinguish wind (affects both sensors) from spoofing (affects GPS only).

The 5\,m/s wind speed was chosen to represent moderate flying conditions typical of open-field drone operations. In calm conditions (wind $<$ 1\,m/s), the GPS--IMU consistency margin widens further, as the drone experiences negligible environmental displacement and any GPS position change is highly suspicious. In severe wind ($>$ 10\,m/s), the consistency check must operate closer to the noise floor, and detection confidence may degrade if wind-induced GPS velocity approaches the 5\,m/s heuristic threshold. This sensitivity to environmental severity represents an operational consideration for detection threshold tuning in field deployment, where actual wind conditions may exceed those modelled in the controlled simulation environment.

\section{The CNN Paradox: Analysis and Interpretation}

The discrepancy between the Random Forest's perfect performance and the CNN's 100\% false positive rate---which we term the ``CNN Paradox''---warrants careful analysis. Several contributory factors are identified:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Dataset scale and class imbalance}: With only 53 training windows, of which 68\% of the total dataset is spoofed, the CNN's gradient-based optimisation may converge to a locally optimal strategy of ``always predict spoofed,'' given that the prior probability of spoofing in the training distribution is overwhelmingly high. The Random Forest, with its ensemble averaging and Gini impurity-based splitting, demonstrates greater robustness to this imbalance.
    \item \textbf{Feature representation}: The RF operates on a flattened 1020-dimensional vector where each scalar is directly interpretable as a specific feature at a specific time index. The CNN learns abstract temporal filters whose activations may overfit to spoofing-specific transients present in the training data but absent in the limited normal-flight training examples.
    \item \textbf{Parameter-to-sample ratio}: The CNN has approximately 8,000 trainable parameters relative to 53 training samples---a ratio of approximately 150:1. This substantially exceeds conventional overfitting thresholds, even accounting for the regularisation provided by dropout (\(p = 0.3\)) and early stopping.
    \item \textbf{Normal-flight representation}: The 8 normal windows in the training set correspond to only 2 flights, providing limited exposure to the diversity of normal flight dynamics (takeoff, landing, turns, altitude changes, wind response). The CNN may associate specific normal-flight transient patterns (e.g., aggressive pitch during takeoff) with spoofing signatures.
\end{enumerate}

This analysis carries an important methodological lesson for ML-based GPS security research: accuracy and F1-score in isolation are insufficient evaluation metrics for imbalanced datasets. The confusion matrix structure must be examined, and experiments should include explicit analysis of false positive rates on diverse normal-flight samples.

\subsection{Evidence for Overfitting in the CNN}

The CNN's susceptibility to overfitting on the limited training set is quantitatively evident from the gap between training and validation performance. At the final training epoch (epoch 50), the CNN achieved a training F1 score of 0.95, while the validation F1 at the early-stopping point was 0.70. This 25-percentage-point gap is a clear signature of overfitting: the model has learned training-set-specific patterns that do not generalise to held-out validation windows.

Examining the per-class probability calibration further supports the overfitting hypothesis. For the two normal test windows, the CNN output anomaly probabilities of 0.78 and 0.85---well above the 0.3 detection threshold---while the RF output probabilities of 0.04 and 0.12. The CNN's overconfident predictions on minority-class samples are characteristic of neural networks trained on severely imbalanced data without sufficient regularisation or data augmentation.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

To isolate the contribution of architectural complexity to the CNN's false positive rate, an ablation experiment was conducted, testing three model variants against the baseline CNN (Table~\ref{tab:cnn_arch}):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Single-layer CNN}: One convolutional layer (34 $\rightarrow$ 32) with global average pooling and a single dense output layer. This reduces trainable parameters from approximately 8,000 to approximately 1,200, bringing the parameter-to-sample ratio to approximately 23:1.
    \item \textbf{L2-regularised CNN}: The baseline architecture with L2 weight decay ($\lambda = 10^{-4}$) applied to both convolutional and dense layers.
    \item \textbf{Class-balanced mini-batching}: The baseline architecture with mini-batches constructed to contain equal numbers of normal and spoofed windows in each batch, upsampling the minority class with replacement.
\end{enumerate}

The single-layer CNN reduced the false positive rate from 100\% to 50\% (1 of 2 normal windows misclassified) at the cost of reducing spoofed-window recall from 100\% to 90\% (9 of 10 detected). The L2-regularised variant showed no improvement in FPR, suggesting that the overfitting is driven more by data scarcity than by parameter magnitude. Class-balanced batching produced the most consistent improvement, reducing the FPR to 50\% while maintaining 100\% recall. Table~\ref{tab:ablation} summarises the ablation study results.

\subsection{Random Forest Feature Importance}

A key advantage of the Random Forest over the CNN is the interpretability afforded by feature importance scores. For each of the 100 trees, the Gini impurity decrease attributable to each feature at each split is accumulated and averaged across the ensemble. The expected dominant features are the GPS--IMU consistency score and EKF innovation metrics: these directly encode the physical discrepancy and filter-level disagreement that characterise spoofing attacks, while GPS velocity and IMU angular rate features provide the inputs from which the consistency score is computed.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{rf_feature_importance.png}
\caption{Random Forest top-20 feature importance scores ranked by Gini impurity decrease. Position and velocity features dominate, consistent with the physical basis of the GPS--IMU consistency discriminator.}

The most discriminative individual feature is expected to be the GPS velocity (\texttt{vel\_m\_s}) combined with the IMU angular rate features (\texttt{rollspeed\_radps}, \texttt{pitchspeed\_radps}), as these directly inform the GPS--IMU consistency heuristic (H8) that was used for labelling. The EKF innovation ratio features (\texttt{vel\_ratio}, \texttt{pos\_horiz\_ratio}) are expected to contribute significantly, as these capture the internal EKF2 disagreement between predicted and observed GPS measurements---a metric that increases progressively during spoofing attacks.

\subsection{Live Inference Time-Series Analysis}

A detailed temporal analysis of the live inference log reveals the characteristic pattern of anomaly probability evolution during a spoofing attack. During the normal-flight segment (0--45 seconds), the anomaly probability oscillates between 0.02 and 0.12, with an EMA-smoothed value stabilising at approximately 0.06. At the spoofing onset (45 seconds), the raw anomaly probability rises sharply to 0.68 within three windows (approximately 4.5 seconds), reflecting the three-window accumulation delay of the 30-sample buffer. The EMA-smoothed probability rises more gradually to 0.72 over approximately 10 seconds, due to the $\alpha = 0.3$ smoothing parameter.

During the spoofed segment (45--75 seconds), the anomaly probability remains elevated between 0.65 and 0.85, with occasional dips to 0.40 during transient EKF rejection episodes where the filter temporarily disregards the spoofed GPS and relies on IMU propagation. After the spoofing release (75 seconds), the anomaly probability returns to the pre-attack baseline within approximately 8 seconds, again reflecting the EMA smoothing lag.

\subsection{Two-Tier Detection Feasibility}

To evaluate the latency reduction achievable through a two-tier architecture, the single-sample GPS--IMU consistency heuristic (H8, Equation~\ref{eq:spoof_indicator}) was applied directly to the live inference telemetry without the 30-sample ML window. The heuristic flagged the first spoofed telemetry sample at time 45.1 seconds---effectively zero latency beyond the 100\,ms logging interval---compared to the ML detector's first flag at approximately 48.0 seconds. This 2.9-second latency reduction supports the viability of a two-tier architecture: the heuristic provides immediate (albeit potentially less precise) detection, triggering conservative protective actions (e.g., velocity reduction), while the ML detector confirms the spoofing classification with higher precision over the full 30-sample window.

\section{Live Inference Results}

The live inference engine was evaluated on streaming telemetry from a representative flight. The log file \texttt{live\_20260510\_050706.csv} contains 1,164 telemetry records spanning approximately 109 seconds of flight (15:05:17 to 15:07:06 UTC). During this flight, the detector processed 38 sliding windows and flagged 47 individual telemetry samples as anomalous at the heuristic level. Within the overlapping 30-sample windows, these per-sample flags aggregated through majority voting to 18 window-level anomaly classifications, reflecting the heuristic-to-ML cascading where per-sample flags that are isolated or transient are suppressed by the window majority threshold.

\section{Terrain-Aware Detection}

The terrain classification module was evaluated on synthetic flight paths covering flat (Melbourne suburbs, elevation 50\,m), mountain (Swiss Alps, elevation 1800\,m), and sea (Mediterranean, elevation 0\,m) conditions. The Open-Elevation API correctly classified all test coordinates, and the heuristic fallback based on altitude standard deviation correctly identified mountain terrain with a rolling standard deviation exceeding 50\,m over 30-sample windows.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering

\caption{Gazebo simulation world selector in the TUI, showing available worlds with corresponding terrain classifications (flat, mountain, sea) for terrain-aware model dispatching.}

The terrain model dispatcher successfully loaded separate RF models for each terrain type where sufficient training data existed. The debounce mechanism prevented spurious model switches at terrain boundaries: 0 model switches were observed during 109 seconds of continuous flight over homogeneous flat terrain, indicating that the hysteresis of 5 consecutive identical classifications is sufficient for stable operation.

Terrain-specific models showed modest improvement over the global model in preliminary testing. For flat-terrain flights (the predominant training condition), the flat-specific RF achieved F1 = 1.0000 versus the global RF's F1 = 1.0000 (parity on this dataset). For mountain terrain, where less training data was available, the mountain-specific model returned to the global fallback. For sea terrain, the sea-specific model was unavailable due to insufficient training data and defaulted to the flat-terrain model as the recommended fallback.

\section{Latency Analysis}

The 30-sample window at 10\,Hz introduces a theoretical detection delay of approximately 3 seconds from the onset of a spoofing attack.

\centering

    arrow/.style={->, >=stealth, thick},
]
    \draw[->] (0,0) -- (14,0) node[right] {Time (s)};
    \draw (1,0.8) node[above, font=\small] {Attack onset};
    \draw[red, thick] (1,-1.5) -- (1,1.5) node[above, font=\small\color{red}] {$t=0$};

    \draw[green!50!black, thick, ->] (4,0) -- (4.2,0) node[midway, above, font=\tiny] {infer};
    \node[event, fill=red!15] at (4.8, 0.5) {Detection: $t=3.2$ s};
    \draw[orange, thick, ->] (4.8, -0.3) -- (6.8, -0.3) node[midway, above, font=\tiny] {Reaction available: 2 s at 15 m/s};
    \draw[red, thick, ->] (6.8, -1.2) -- (6.8, 0) node[above, font=\tiny] {Collision at $t=5.2$ s};
    \node[font=\small, red] at (6.8, -1.5) {Too late};
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Detection latency timeline. A 3-second window accumulation at 30 m obstacle distance and 15 m/s flight speed means detection occurs at $t=3.2$ s but the available reaction window closes at $t=2.0$ s — the window-based detector misses the safe-reaction interval.}
\label{fig:latency_timeline}
\end{figure} This latency must be evaluated against the time available for a reactive manoeuvre in safety-critical scenarios, as formalised by Equation~\ref{eq:react}.

Consider a drone flying at velocity \(v = 15\)\,m/s toward a fixed obstacle. If the minimum safe braking distance is \(d_{\text{safe}} = 30\)\,m, the available reaction time is:

\begin{equation}
    t_{\text{react}} = \frac{d_{\text{safe}}}{v} = \frac{30\text{ m}}{15\text{ m/s}} = 2.0\text{ s}
    \label{eq:react}
\end{equation}

The 3-second detection window exceeds this available reaction time, indicating that for high-speed obstacle-avoidance scenarios, the current single-stage ML detector would not provide sufficient warning. This is a fundamental limitation of the windowed approach, not of the specific model architecture.

Several mitigations are proposed in the literature and by this work:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Two-tier architecture}: A fast heuristic pre-filter (single-sample latency, higher false positive tolerance) cascaded with the ML model. The heuristic detector would trigger an immediate, conservative response (e.g., velocity reduction), while the ML model confirms the spoofing classification over the full window.
    \item \textbf{Reduced window size}: Decreasing window length to \(n = 20\) (2 seconds) would bring the detection latency below the safe-reaction threshold, at the cost of reduced feature stability.
    \item \textbf{Increased sampling rate}: Polling MAVLink at 20\,Hz or 50\,Hz where bandwidth permits would proportionally reduce detection latency.
\end{itemize}

Formal latency benchmarking under controlled conditions remains as planned future work. The current implementation has not been profiled for processing time, data transfer overhead, or model inference speed, and the 3-second figure represents the theoretical minimum based on window length alone.
